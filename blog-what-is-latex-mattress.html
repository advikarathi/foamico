What Is a Latex Mattress?

February 18, 2025Foamico Sleep Experts7 min read

Latex is one of the oldest mattress technologies around, and also one of the most misunderstood. Here's what it actually is, how it's made, and whether it's worth the higher price tag.

Where latex comes from

Natural latex starts as sap tapped from the rubber tree (Hevea brasiliensis), then whipped into a froth, poured into a mould, and vulcanised. Unlike memory foam, latex responds to pressure rather than heat — press down and it compresses immediately, then springs back the instant the pressure is released.

Natural vs. synthetic vs. blended

Natural latex is eco-friendly, hypoallergenic, and the longest-lasting of the three — typically 15–20+ years — but also the most expensive and the heaviest to move. Synthetic latex is petrochemical-based, cheaper, but shorter-lived and can carry a stronger odour. Blended latex mixes the two, usually mostly synthetic, as a middle ground on price and performance. It's worth asking directly what percentage is natural, since labelling in this space isn't always precise.

Dunlop vs. Talalay: the two manufacturing methods

The Dunlop process pours latex all at once into a mould before baking, which lets denser particles settle — the result is firmer, heavier, and typically used for support-core layers. The Talalay process adds a vacuum and flash-freeze step for a more uniform, softer, more breathable result — usually used for the comfort layer on top. Many good latex mattresses combine both: a firm Dunlop base with a softer Talalay top.

What it feels like

Buoyant is the best word for it — a soft initial give followed immediately by a firm push back up, rather than the slow sink of memory foam. Sleepers who change position often tend to prefer this responsiveness over the "stuck in place" feeling of dense foam.

Benefits

  • Long lifespan — often 2–3x that of memory foam
  • Naturally hypoallergenic, resists dust mites and mould
  • Breathable and noticeably cooler than memory foam
  • Instant responsiveness for position changes

Drawbacks

  • Higher upfront cost than foam alternatives
  • Heavy — harder to rotate or move than foam
  • A small number of people have latex sensitivities and should check with a doctor first

Who it suits

Hot sleepers, anyone who values eco-conscious materials, frequent position-changers, and people with dust or allergy sensitivities. If budget or ease of moving the mattress matters more, an HR foam build like Resto gets you some of the same responsive support at a lower price and weight.
